5  Iterative Methods for Linear Systems

\[ \newcommand{\R}{\mathbb{R}} \newcommand{\C}{\mathbb{C}} \newcommand{\N}{\mathbb{N}} \newcommand{\E}{\mathbb{E}} \newcommand{\eps}{\varepsilon} \newcommand{\abs}[1]{\left\lvert #1 \right\rvert} \newcommand{\norm}[1]{\left\lVert #1 \right\rVert} \newcommand{\ninf}[1]{\left\lVert #1 \right\rVert_{\infty}} \newcommand{\ntwo}[1]{\left\lVert #1 \right\rVert_{2}} \newcommand{\none}[1]{\left\lVert #1 \right\rVert_{1}} \newcommand{\inner}[2]{\left\langle #1,\, #2 \right\rangle} \newcommand{\bigO}{\mathcal{O}} \newcommand{\dd}{\mathrm{d}} \newcommand{\diff}[2]{\frac{\mathrm{d} #1}{\mathrm{d} #2}} \newcommand{\pdiff}[2]{\frac{\partial #1}{\partial #2}} \newcommand{\spec}{\rho} \newcommand{\cond}{\kappa} \newcommand{\Span}{\operatorname{span}} \newcommand{\rank}{\operatorname{rank}} \newcommand{\tr}{\operatorname{tr}} \newcommand{\diag}{\operatorname{diag}} \newcommand{\argmax}{\operatorname*{arg\,max}} \newcommand{\argmin}{\operatorname*{arg\,min}} \newcommand{\defeq}{:=} \]

The direct methods of Chapter 4 solve \(Ax=b\) exactly (up to rounding) in a fixed \(\bigO(n^3)\) number of operations. For the large, sparse systems that arise from discretized differential equations that cost is prohibitive, and it throws away the one piece of structure we have: multiplying by \(A\) is cheap, often \(\bigO(n)\), even when factoring \(A\) is not. Iterative methods exploit exactly this. They never touch the entries of \(A\) except through matrix–vector products (they are matrix-free), they produce a sequence \(x^{(0)},x^{(1)},\dots\) that we hope converges to the solution, and we stop as soon as the approximation is good enough. The aim is to trade the \(\bigO(n^3)\) of a factorization for \(\bigO(n)\) per iteration and few enough iterations to win.

To speak of convergence we first need to measure the size of vectors and matrices, so the chapter opens with norms and the spectral radius, then builds the two classical stationary iterations (Jacobi and Gauss–Seidel) and their relaxed variant (SOR), analyzes when they converge, and turns to the sensitivity of \(Ax=b\) through the condition number. The second half specializes to symmetric positive definite systems, where solving \(Ax=b\) becomes a minimization problem and the steepest descent and conjugate gradient methods emerge, closing with the convergence rate of conjugate gradient and the idea of preconditioning.

5.1 Vector norms

A norm turns the vector space \(\R^n\) into a metric space, giving us the language of distance and convergence.

Definition 5.1 (Vector norm) A vector norm on \(\R^n\) is a function \(\norm{\cdot}:\R^n\to\R\) such that for all \(x,y\in\R^n\) and \(\alpha\in\R\):

  1. \(\norm{x}\ge 0\), with \(\norm{x}=0\) if and only if \(x=0\) (nonnegativity and definiteness);
  2. \(\norm{\alpha x}=\abs{\alpha}\,\norm{x}\) (homogeneity);
  3. \(\norm{x+y}\le\norm{x}+\norm{y}\) (triangle inequality).

Writing \(x=(x_1,\dots,x_n)^\top\), the three norms we use throughout are the \(2\)-norm (or Euclidean norm), the \(\infty\)-norm (or max norm), and the \(1\)-norm, \[ \ntwo{x}=\Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2\Bigr)^{1/2},\qquad \ninf{x}=\max_{1\le i\le n}\abs{x_i},\qquad \none{x}=\sum_{i=1}^n\abs{x_i}, \tag{5.1}\] all special cases of the \(p\)-norm \(\norm{x}_p=(\sum_i\abs{x_i}^p)^{1/p}\). The \(2\)-norm is special: it comes from the inner product \(\inner{x}{y}=x^\top y\) through \(\ntwo{x}=\sqrt{\inner{x}{x}}\), and that extra structure is what makes its triangle inequality subtle. For the \(1\)- and \(\infty\)-norms the triangle inequality follows termwise from \(\abs{a+b}\le\abs{a}+\abs{b}\); for the \(2\)-norm it rests on the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality.

Theorem 5.1 (Cauchy–Schwarz inequality) For all \(x,y\in\R^n\), \(\;\abs{x^\top y}\le\ntwo{x}\,\ntwo{y}\).

Proof. If \(y=0\) both sides vanish, so assume \(x,y\ne 0\). For any \(\lambda\in\R\), expanding the nonnegative quantity \(\ntwo{x-\lambda y}^2\) gives \[ 0\le\ntwo{x-\lambda y}^2=(x-\lambda y)^\top(x-\lambda y) =\ntwo{x}^2-2\lambda\,(x^\top y)+\lambda^2\ntwo{y}^2 . \] Choosing \(\lambda=\ntwo{x}/\ntwo{y}\) makes the two outer terms equal to \(\ntwo{x}^2\), so the inequality collapses to \(2\ntwo{x}^2-2\lambda\,(x^\top y)\ge 0\), i.e. \(x^\top y\le\ntwo{x}\ntwo{y}\). Applying the same bound to \(-x\) gives the absolute value. \(\square\)

Cauchy–Schwarz yields the triangle inequality for the \(2\)-norm at once: \(\ntwo{x+y}^2=\ntwo{x}^2+2x^\top y+\ntwo{y}^2\le(\ntwo{x}+\ntwo{y})^2\). A sequence \(\{x^{(k)}\}\) converges to \(x\) in a norm if \(\norm{x^{(k)}-x}\to 0\). The reassuring first fact is that convergence in the \(\infty\)-norm is exactly componentwise convergence, since \(\ninf{x^{(k)}-x}\to 0\) if and only if each coordinate \(x_i^{(k)}\to x_i\).

One might worry that the choice of norm changes what “converges” means. On a finite-dimensional space it does not.

Theorem 5.2 (Equivalence of norms) All norms on \(\R^n\) are equivalent: for any two norms \(\norm{\cdot}_a\) and \(\norm{\cdot}_b\) there are constants \(0<c\le C\) with \(c\,\norm{x}_a\le\norm{x}_b\le C\,\norm{x}_a\) for all \(x\). Consequently a sequence converges in one norm if and only if it converges in every norm, and to the same limit, so we may write \(\lim_{k\to\infty}x^{(k)}\) unambiguously.

Proof. This is a standard compactness result (a continuous positive function attains a positive minimum on the unit sphere), stated here without full proof. A concrete instance we use directly is \[ \ninf{x}\le\ntwo{x}\le\sqrt{n}\,\ninf{x}, \tag{5.2}\] whose right inequality is \(\ntwo{x}^2=\sum_i x_i^2\le n\,(\max_j\abs{x_j})^2=n\,\ninf{x}^2\) and whose left inequality picks out a single maximizing coordinate. \(\square\)

5.2 Matrix norms

To measure a matrix we ask how much it can stretch a vector. Any function \(\norm{\cdot}:\R^{n\times n}\to\R\) satisfying nonnegativity/definiteness, homogeneity, and the triangle inequality is a candidate, but matrices compose, so we demand one more property.

Definition 5.2 (Matrix norm) A matrix norm on \(\R^{n\times n}\) is a function \(\norm{\cdot}\) obeying the three vector-norm axioms together with submultiplicativity, \(\norm{AB}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{B}\).

The most useful matrix norms are those induced by a vector norm: they measure the largest factor by which \(A\) can lengthen a unit vector.

Definition 5.3 (Induced (natural) matrix norm) Given a vector norm \(\norm{\cdot}\) on \(\R^n\), the induced (or natural) matrix norm is \[ \norm{A}=\max_{\norm{x}=1}\norm{Ax}=\max_{z\ne 0}\frac{\norm{Az}}{\norm{z}} . \tag{5.3}\]

The two forms in Equation 5.3 agree by homogeneity (\(z=x/\norm{x}\)). By its very definition an induced norm satisfies \(\norm{Az}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{z}\) for every \(z\), and this is what makes it submultiplicative: \(\norm{ABz}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{Bz}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{B}\,\norm{z}\) for all \(z\), so \(\norm{AB}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{B}\) (and the bound chains, e.g. \(\norm{ABCz}\le\norm{A}\norm{B}\norm{C}\norm{z}\)). We write \(\norm{A}_p\) for the norm induced by the vector \(p\)-norm. The \(\infty\)-norm has a clean closed form as the maximum absolute row sum.

Theorem 5.3 (Infinity matrix norm) For \(A=(a_{ij})\), \(\;\displaystyle\ninf{A}=\max_{1\le i\le n}\sum_{j=1}^n\abs{a_{ij}}\).

Proof. Set \(C=\max_i\sum_j\abs{a_{ij}}\). For any \(x\) with \(\ninf{x}\le 1\), \[ \ninf{Ax}=\max_i\Bigl|\sum_j a_{ij}x_j\Bigr|\le\max_i\sum_j\abs{a_{ij}}\,\abs{x_j} \le\max_i\sum_j\abs{a_{ij}}\;\ninf{x}=C\,\ninf{x}, \] so \(\ninf{A}\le C\). For the reverse, let \(i\) be a row attaining the maximum and set \(x_j=\operatorname{sign}(a_{ij})\), a unit \(\infty\)-norm vector; then the \(i\)-th component of \(Ax\) is \(\sum_j a_{ij}\operatorname{sign}(a_{ij})=\sum_j\abs{a_{ij}}=C\), so \(\ninf{Ax}\ge C\) and hence \(\ninf{A}\ge C\). Therefore \(\ninf{A}=C\). \(\square\)

The Euclidean matrix norm has no such elementary formula; it is the largest singular value, \[ \ntwo{A}=\sqrt{\spec(A^\top A)}=\sigma_1(A), \tag{5.4}\] where \(\spec(\cdot)\) is the spectral radius of the next section. To see why, note \(\ntwo{A}^2=\max_{\norm{x}=1}x^\top(A^\top A)x\); by the Courant–Fischer maximum principle applied to the symmetric positive-semidefinite matrix \(A^\top A\) this maximum is its largest eigenvalue, and since an SVD \(A=U\Sigma V^\top\) gives \(A^\top A=V\Sigma^2 V^\top\), that eigenvalue is \(\sigma_1(A)^2\). We take Equation 5.4 on faith here; the eigen/singular-value machinery behind it is developed in Chapter 7.

Important

Not every “matrix norm” is induced. Stacking the entries of \(A\) into a long vector and applying a vector \(p\)-norm inherits every axiom except submultiplicativity — except in one case, the Frobenius norm \(\norm{A}_F=(\sum_{ij}a_{ij}^2)^{1/2}=\sqrt{\tr(A^\top A)}\), which is submultiplicative and hence a genuine matrix norm, yet is not induced by any vector norm. In particular \(\norm{A}_F\ne\ntwo{A}\); do not conflate them.

5.3 Spectral radius

Convergence of every stationary iteration below is governed by a single number attached to a matrix: the largest magnitude of its eigenvalues. Recall \(\lambda\in\C\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) if \(Ax=\lambda x\) for some \(x\ne 0\); the eigenvalues are the roots of the characteristic polynomial \(\det(A-\lambda I)\) and may be complex even for real \(A\).

Definition 5.4 (Spectral radius) The spectral radius of \(A\in\R^{n\times n}\) is \[ \spec(A)=\max\bigl\{\,\abs{\lambda}\;:\;\lambda\text{ an eigenvalue of }A\,\bigr\}, \] the maximum taken over all (possibly complex) eigenvalues.

The spectral radius is never larger than any induced norm, and it is the exact rate at which powers of \(A\) grow or decay.

Proposition 5.1 (Spectral radius bounds) For any induced matrix norm, \(\spec(A)\le\norm{A}\).

Proof. Let \(\lambda\) attain the spectral radius, with eigenvector \(x\ne 0\), and let \(X\) be the matrix whose columns are all \(x\), so \(AX=\lambda X\) and \(X\ne 0\). Taking any induced norm and using homogeneity and submultiplicativity, \(\abs{\lambda}\,\norm{X}=\norm{AX}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{X}\); dividing by \(\norm{X}>0\) gives \(\abs{\lambda}\le\norm{A}\), hence \(\spec(A)\le\norm{A}\). If \(\lambda\) is complex the eigenvector \(x\) (and hence \(X\)) is complex; the same estimate goes through verbatim once the induced norm is extended to \(\C^{n\times n}\) in the usual way, so no generality is lost. \(\square\)

Definition 5.5 (Convergent matrix) A matrix \(A\) is convergent if \(\lim_{k\to\infty}(A^k)_{ij}=0\) for all \(i,j\), equivalently (by equivalence of norms on \(\R^{n\times n}\)) if \(\norm{A^k}\to 0\) for some — hence every — matrix norm.

The following equivalence is the analytic heart of the chapter: it converts “the iteration converges” into the checkable condition \(\spec(T)<1\).

Theorem 5.4 (Characterization of convergent matrices) For \(A\in\R^{n\times n}\) the following are equivalent: (i) \(A\) is convergent; (ii) \(\lim_{k\to\infty}\norm{A^k}=0\) for some (equivalently every) matrix norm; (iii) \(\spec(A)<1\); (iv) \(\lim_{k\to\infty}A^k x=0\) for every \(x\in\R^n\).

Proof. Equivalence of (i), (ii), (iv) is the equivalence of norms applied to the vectorized powers, together with the observations that (iv)\(\Rightarrow\)(i) by plugging in the standard basis vectors \(e_j\), and (i)\(\Rightarrow\)(iv) by expanding \(A^kx\) coordinatewise and taking limits. That \(\spec(A)<1\) is equivalent to convergence is the standard spectral criterion (via the Jordan form, \(\norm{A^k}\to 0\) iff every eigenvalue has modulus \(<1\)); we take this direction as known. The one-line intuition: if some \(\abs{\lambda}\ge 1\) then \(A^k\) does not shrink the corresponding eigenvector, while if all \(\abs{\lambda}<1\) the powers decay geometrically at rate \(\spec(A)\). \(\square\)

5.4 The Jacobi method

We now build the first iteration for \(Ax=b\). Split \(A\) into its diagonal, strictly lower, and strictly upper triangular parts, \[ A=D-L-U, \tag{5.5}\] where \(D=\diag(a_{11},\dots,a_{nn})\), \(-L\) is the strictly lower part, and \(-U\) the strictly upper part. (Beware: these \(L,U\) are not the factors of an \(LU\) factorization from Chapter 4; they are fixed pieces of \(A\), not computed.) Assume every diagonal entry \(a_{ii}\ne 0\), so \(D\) is invertible.

Rewriting \(Ax=b\) as \((D-L-U)x=b\), i.e. \(Dx=b+(L+U)x\), exhibits the solution as a fixed point of the map \[ F(x)=D^{-1}\bigl[b+(L+U)x\bigr] . \] The Jacobi method is the fixed-point iteration \(x^{(k+1)}=F(x^{(k)})\): pick \(x^{(0)}\) and iterate. If the iterates converge, continuity of \(F\) forces the limit to satisfy \(x^\star=F(x^\star)\), i.e. to solve \(Ax=b\). Whether they converge is the subject of Section 5.6.

NoteAlgorithm — Jacobi iteration

Given \(A\) (with \(a_{ii}\ne 0\)), \(b\), and \(x^{(0)}\), iterate for \(k=0,1,\dots\) \[ x^{(k+1)}=D^{-1}\bigl[b+(L+U)x^{(k)}\bigr], \qquad\text{componentwise}\qquad x_i^{(k+1)}=\frac{1}{a_{ii}}\Bigl(b_i-\sum_{\substack{j=1\\ j\ne i}}^{n}a_{ij}x_j^{(k)}\Bigr). \tag{5.6}\] Each new component uses only old components \(x_j^{(k)}\), so the updates are independent and the sweep parallelizes trivially.

5.5 The Gauss–Seidel method

Jacobi computes an entire new vector from the old one. But while sweeping \(i=1,\dots,n\) we have already improved \(x_1^{(k+1)},\dots,x_{i-1}^{(k+1)}\) by the time we reach component \(i\) — why not use them? Grouping the splitting Equation 5.5 as \((D-L)x=b+Ux\) instead does exactly that, suggesting the iteration \[ x^{(k+1)}=(D-L)^{-1}\bigl[b+Ux^{(k)}\bigr], \tag{5.7}\] the Gauss–Seidel method. Implementing it does not require inverting \(D-L\): it is the lower-triangular system \((D-L)x^{(k+1)}=b+Ux^{(k)}\), solved by forward substitution. Expanding the \(i\)-th equation \(a_{ii}x_i^{(k+1)}+\sum_{j<i}a_{ij}x_j^{(k+1)}=b_i-\sum_{j>i}a_{ij}x_j^{(k)}\) and solving for \(x_i^{(k+1)}\) gives the in-place formula.

NoteAlgorithm — Gauss–Seidel iteration

Given \(A\) (with \(a_{ii}\ne 0\)), \(b\), and \(x^{(0)}\), iterate for \(k=0,1,\dots\) and \(i=1,\dots,n\) in order \[ x_i^{(k+1)}=\frac{1}{a_{ii}}\Bigl(b_i-\sum_{j=1}^{i-1}a_{ij}x_j^{(k+1)} -\sum_{j=i+1}^{n}a_{ij}x_j^{(k)}\Bigr). \tag{5.8}\] The right-hand side uses the already updated \(x_j^{(k+1)}\) for \(j<i\), so components are computed sequentially with no extra work — like forward substitution for a triangular matrix.

Jacobi and Gauss–Seidel cost the same number of flops per iteration (ignoring parallelism); which one converges in fewer iterations has no clean general answer, though sufficient conditions for Gauss–Seidel to win are given by the Stein–Rosenberg theorem (see the textbook).

5.6 Convergence of Jacobi and Gauss–Seidel

Both iterations have the stationary form \[ x^{(k+1)}=Tx^{(k)}+c, \tag{5.9}\] with iteration matrix \(T\) and constant \(c\) read off from Equation 5.6 and Equation 5.7: \[ \text{Jacobi:}\quad T=D^{-1}(L+U),\ \ c=D^{-1}b;\qquad \text{Gauss–Seidel:}\quad T=(D-L)^{-1}U,\ \ c=(D-L)^{-1}b . \tag{5.10}\] The convergence of any iteration of the form Equation 5.9 depends only on \(T\), through the spectral criterion of Theorem 5.4.

Theorem 5.5 (Convergence of stationary iterations) Let \(T\in\R^{n\times n}\) and \(c\in\R^n\). The iteration \(x^{(k+1)}=Tx^{(k)}+c\) converges to the unique solution of \(x=Tx+c\) for every starting point \(x^{(0)}\) and every \(c\) if and only if \[ \spec(T)<1 . \]

Proof. Suppose \(\spec(T)<1\). Then \(1\) is not an eigenvalue of \(T\), so \(I-T\) is invertible and \(x=Tx+c\) has the unique solution \(x=(I-T)^{-1}c\). Define the error \(y^{(k)}=x^{(k)}-x\). Subtracting \(x=Tx+c\) from \(x^{(k+1)}=Tx^{(k)}+c\) gives \[ y^{(k+1)}=x^{(k+1)}-x=T x^{(k)}-Tx=T\,y^{(k)}, \] hence \(y^{(k)}=T^k y^{(0)}\). By Theorem 5.4, \(\spec(T)<1\) makes \(T\) convergent, so \(y^{(k)}=T^k y^{(0)}\to 0\), i.e. \(x^{(k)}\to x\) for every \(x^{(0)}\).

Conversely, suppose the iteration converges for every \(x^{(0)}\) and every \(c\). Take \(c=0\): the fixed point is \(x=0\) and \(x^{(k)}=T^k x^{(0)}\to 0\) for every \(x^{(0)}\), which is precisely condition (iv) of Theorem 5.4. Hence \(T\) is convergent and \(\spec(T)<1\). \(\square\)

When an induced norm of \(T\) is already below \(1\) we get convergence and a quantitative rate, directly from \(\spec(T)\le\norm{T}\) (Proposition 5.1).

Corollary 5.1 (Error estimates) If \(\norm{T}<1\) for some induced norm, the iteration converges for every \(x^{(0)}\), and \[ \norm{x^{(k)}-x}\le\norm{T}^{k}\,\norm{x^{(0)}-x}, \qquad \norm{x^{(k)}-x}\le\frac{\norm{T}^{k}}{1-\norm{T}}\,\norm{x^{(1)}-x^{(0)}} . \tag{5.11}\]

Proof. The first bound is \(\norm{y^{(k)}}=\norm{T^ky^{(0)}}\le\norm{T}^k\norm{y^{(0)}}\). For the second, telescope \(x^{(k)}-x^{(l)}=\sum_{j=0}^{l-k-1}\bigl(x^{(k+j)}-x^{(k+j+1)}\bigr)\) for \(l>k\) and use \(x^{(k+j)}-x^{(k+j+1)}=T^{k+j}(x^{(0)}-x^{(1)})\) to get \(\norm{x^{(k)}-x^{(l)}}\le\norm{T}^k\bigl(\sum_{j\ge 0}\norm{T}^j\bigr)\norm{x^{(1)}-x^{(0)}}\); letting \(l\to\infty\) and summing the geometric series \(\sum_j\norm{T}^j=1/(1-\norm{T})\) yields the claim. \(\square\)

For Jacobi in the \(\infty\)-norm this criterion becomes a condition on \(A\) alone. With \(T=D^{-1}(L+U)\) we have \(T_{ij}=-a_{ij}/a_{ii}\) for \(i\ne j\) and \(T_{ii}=0\), so by Theorem 5.3, \[ \ninf{T}=\max_i\sum_{j\ne i}\frac{\abs{a_{ij}}}{\abs{a_{ii}}}<1 \iff \abs{a_{ii}}>\sum_{j\ne i}\abs{a_{ij}}\ \text{ for all }i . \]

Definition 5.6 (Strict diagonal dominance) A matrix \(A=(a_{ij})\) is strictly diagonally dominant if \(\abs{a_{ii}}>\sum_{j\ne i}\abs{a_{ij}}\) for every \(i\).

Theorem 5.6 (Convergence under diagonal dominance) If \(A\) is strictly diagonally dominant, then for any \(b\) and any \(x^{(0)}\) both the Jacobi and the Gauss–Seidel iterations converge to the solution of \(Ax=b\).

Proof. For Jacobi the computation above gives \(\ninf{T}<1\), so Corollary 5.1 applies. Gauss–Seidel also converges under the same hypothesis; the standard argument bounds the eigenvalues of \((D-L)^{-1}U\) below \(1\) using diagonal dominance, which we state without proof. \(\square\)

5.7 Relaxation and SOR

Even when Gauss–Seidel converges, its iteration matrix may have spectral radius close to \(1\), so convergence is slow. Relaxation introduces a parameter \(\omega\ne 0\) that scales how far each step moves. Multiplying \(Ax=b\) by \(\omega\) and splitting \(\omega D=D-(1-\omega)D\) turns the system into \(\bigl(D-(1-\omega)D-\omega L-\omega U\bigr)x=\omega b\), which rearranges to \(Dx=\omega\bigl[b+(L+U)x\bigr]+(1-\omega)Dx\) and hence to the weighted Jacobi update \[ x^{(k+1)}=\omega D^{-1}\bigl[b+(L+U)x^{(k)}\bigr]+(1-\omega)x^{(k)} =T_\omega x^{(k)}+c_\omega, \tag{5.12}\] where \(T_\omega=(1-\omega)I+\omega D^{-1}(L+U)=I-\omega D^{-1}A\) (using \(L+U=D-A\)) and \(c_\omega=\omega D^{-1}b\). For \(\omega\in(0,1)\) this is a weighted average of a Jacobi step and “do nothing” (under-relaxation); for \(\omega>1\) it overshoots the Jacobi direction (over-relaxation); \(\omega=1\) recovers Jacobi. Grouping the lower-triangular part with the update instead — rearranging \((D-\omega L)x=\bigl[(1-\omega)D+\omega U\bigr]x+\omega b\) — gives the relaxed Gauss–Seidel iteration, successive over-relaxation (SOR): \[ x^{(k+1)}=(D-\omega L)^{-1}\bigl\{\bigl[(1-\omega)D+\omega U\bigr]x^{(k)}+\omega b\bigr\} =T_\omega x^{(k)}+c_\omega, \tag{5.13}\] now with \(T_\omega=(D-\omega L)^{-1}\bigl[(1-\omega)D+\omega U\bigr]\) and \(c_\omega=\omega(D-\omega L)^{-1}b\). Like every stationary iteration it converges iff \(\spec(T_\omega)<1\), and the parameter \(\omega\) is a knob to make that spectral radius small.

Two facts frame the search for a good \(\omega\). First, \(\omega\) cannot be chosen freely.

Theorem 5.7 (Kahan’s theorem) If \(a_{ii}\ne 0\) for all \(i\), then \(\spec(T_\omega)\ge\abs{\omega-1}\) for SOR. Consequently SOR can converge only if \(\omega\in(0,2)\).

Proof. The determinant of \(T_\omega\) equals the product of its eigenvalues, and a short computation with the triangular factors gives \(\det T_\omega=(1-\omega)^n\); hence \(\spec(T_\omega)^n\ge\abs{\det T_\omega}=\abs{1-\omega}^n\), so \(\spec(T_\omega)\ge\abs{1-\omega}\). This is \(<1\) only for \(\omega\in(0,2)\). (Full computation left as in the source.) \(\square\)

Second, within that window symmetry pins down convergence. Recall a symmetric matrix \(A\) is positive definite, written \(A\succ 0\), if \(x^\top Ax>0\) for all \(x\ne 0\) (see Section 5.9); such \(A\) has a unique positive definite square root \(A^{1/2}\).

Theorem 5.8 (Ostrowski–Reich theorem) If \(A\) is positive definite and \(\omega\in(0,2)\), then SOR converges for every \(b\) and every \(x^{(0)}\).

Proof. Stated without proof (see the textbook). For the special case of weighted Jacobi one shows directly that \(D^{-1}A\) is diagonalizable with positive eigenvalues when \(A\) is positive definite, so the eigenvalues \(1-\omega\lambda\) of \(T_\omega=I-\omega D^{-1}A\) lie in \((-1,1)\) precisely when \(0<\omega<2/\spec(D^{-1}A)\). \(\square\)

Choosing the optimal \(\omega\) is hard in general; a closed-form optimum is known for tridiagonal positive definite matrices (see the textbook).

5.8 Condition numbers and error sensitivity

Iterative methods stop when the residual \(r=b-A\tilde{x}\) of the current guess \(\tilde{x}\) is small. But a small residual is not the same as a small error \(\tilde{x}-x\). Consider \[ \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&0.0001\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}x_1\\x_2\end{pmatrix} =\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad x=(1,0)^\top . \] The guess \(\tilde{x}=(1,10)^\top\) has residual \(r=(0,-0.001)^\top\), tiny, yet the error is enormous. The gap between “residual small” and “error small” is measured by the condition number.

Theorem 5.9 (Residual–error bound) Let \(A\) be invertible, \(Ax=b\), and \(\tilde{x}\) a guess with residual \(r=b-A\tilde{x}\). Then in any induced norm \[ \norm{x-\tilde{x}}\le\norm{r}\,\norm{A^{-1}}, \qquad\text{and, if }x,b\ne 0,\qquad \frac{\norm{x-\tilde{x}}}{\norm{x}}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{A^{-1}}\,\frac{\norm{r}}{\norm{b}} . \tag{5.14}\]

Proof. Since \(x=A^{-1}b\) and \(\tilde{x}=A^{-1}(b-r)\), we get \(x-\tilde{x}=A^{-1}r\), so \(\norm{x-\tilde{x}}\le\norm{A^{-1}}\norm{r}\). For the relative bound, \(b=Ax\) gives \(\norm{b}\le\norm{A}\norm{x}\), i.e. \(1/\norm{x}\le\norm{A}/\norm{b}\); multiplying the two inequalities yields the result. \(\square\)

The amplification factor \(\norm{A}\norm{A^{-1}}\) is exactly the condition number \(\cond(A)\) introduced in 2.

Definition 5.7 (Condition number) The condition number of an invertible \(A\) relative to a norm is \(\cond(A)=\norm{A}\,\norm{A^{-1}}\). A matrix is well-conditioned if \(\cond(A)\approx 1\) and ill-conditioned if \(\cond(A)\gg 1\).

Proposition 5.2 (Condition number is at least one) \(\cond(A)\ge 1\) for every invertible \(A\).

Proof. From \(AA^{-1}=I\) and submultiplicativity, \(1=\norm{I}\le\norm{A}\,\norm{A^{-1}}=\cond(A)\). \(\square\)

For the \(2\)-norm, Equation 5.4 makes \(\cond_2(A)=\sigma_1(A)/\sigma_n(A)\) the ratio of largest to smallest singular value; for symmetric positive definite \(A\) this is the ratio of largest to smallest eigenvalue. This is the quantity that will control the speed of conjugate gradient in Section 5.12.

5.9 Positive definite systems

The rest of the chapter specializes to symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrices, the setting where the sharpest iterative methods live.

Definition 5.8 (Positive (semi)definite matrix) A symmetric \(A\) is positive definite (\(A\succ 0\)) if \(x^\top Ax>0\) for all \(x\ne 0\), and positive semidefinite (\(A\succeq 0\)) if \(x^\top Ax\ge 0\) for all \(x\).

The following characterizations are used repeatedly; each is standard linear algebra (Chapter 7 develops the eigenvalue side).

Proposition 5.3 (Characterizations of positive definiteness) For symmetric \(A\) the following are equivalent: (i) \(A\succ 0\); (ii) \(A=B^\top B\) for some invertible \(B\); (iii) \(A\) has strictly positive eigenvalues; (iv) \(A\) has a unique positive definite square root \(A^{1/2}\). When \(A\succ 0\), the spectral theorem gives \(A=U\Lambda U^\top\) with \(U\) orthogonal and \(\Lambda\succ 0\) diagonal, and \(A^{1/2}=U\Lambda^{1/2}U^\top\).

The key structural fact is that solving an SPD system is the same problem as minimizing a quadratic. This is what opens the door to optimization methods.

Theorem 5.10 (SPD solve as minimization) Let \(A\succ 0\). The unique solution \(x\) of \(Ax=b\) is the unique minimizer of the quadratic \[ g(x)=\tfrac12\,x^\top Ax-b^\top x . \tag{5.15}\]

Proof. Using \(\nabla(x^\top Cx)=2Cx\) for symmetric \(C\) and \(\nabla(a^\top x)=a\), the gradient is \[ \nabla g(x)=Ax-b, \] so \(\nabla g(x)=0\) iff \(Ax=b\): the unique critical point is the solution. The Hessian is \(\nabla^2 g\equiv A\succ 0\), so \(g\) is strictly convex and its only critical point is the global minimizer. \(\square\)

TipAny linear system reduces to an SPD one

For a general invertible \(B\), the least-squares reformulation \(\min_x\ntwo{Bx-c}^2\) has gradient condition \(B^\top Bx=B^\top c\), the normal equations, whose matrix \(B^\top B\succ 0\). So “once you can solve SPD systems, you can solve every system.” For a square invertible \(B\) solving \(Bx=c\) directly is usually better, but for rectangular \(B\in\R^{m\times n}\) with full column rank the normal equations are the natural route (revisited in Chapter 6).

The graph of \(g\) is a bowl whose level sets are ellipsoids; their eccentricity is set by \(\cond_2(A)\). A well-conditioned \(A\) gives nearly circular level sets, an ill-conditioned \(A\) gives long thin valleys — the geometry that makes naive descent struggle, as we see next.

5.10 Steepest descent

To minimize \(g\) we can walk downhill. Given the current guess \(x^{(k-1)}\), move along the direction of steepest descent and pick the step length optimally, \[ x^{(k)}=x^{(k-1)}+t^{(k)}v^{(k)} . \] Multivariable calculus says the steepest-descent direction is the negative gradient, which here is exactly the residual, \[ v^{(k)}=-\nabla g(x^{(k-1)})=b-Ax^{(k-1)}=:r^{(k-1)} . \tag{5.16}\] Given a search direction \(v\), the optimal step solves the one-variable problem \(\min_t g(x^{(k-1)}+t v)\). Writing \(x=x^{(k-1)}\), \(r=r^{(k-1)}\), \(v=v^{(k)}\) and expanding, \[ h(t)=g(x+tv)=\tfrac{t^2}{2}v^\top Av+t\,(v^\top Ax)-t\,(v^\top b)+g(x), \] so \(h'(t)=0\) gives the exact line-search step \[ t^{(k)}=\frac{v^\top(b-Ax)}{v^\top Av} =\frac{\inner{v^{(k)}}{r^{(k-1)}}}{\inner{v^{(k)}}{Av^{(k)}}} . \tag{5.17}\] This formula for \(t^{(k)}\) holds for any direction \(v^{(k)}\), a fact the conjugate gradient method will reuse; for steepest descent specifically \(v^{(k)}=r^{(k-1)}\).

NoteAlgorithm — Steepest descent

Given \(x^{(0)}\), \(b\), matvecs by \(A\succ 0\), a cap \(m\), and a relative tolerance \(\eps\): set \(r^{(0)}=b-Ax^{(0)}\); for \(k=1,2,\dots,m\):

  1. \(v^{(k)}=r^{(k-1)}\);
  2. \(t^{(k)}=\dfrac{\inner{v^{(k)}}{r^{(k-1)}}}{\inner{v^{(k)}}{Av^{(k)}}}\);
  3. \(x^{(k)}=x^{(k-1)}+t^{(k)}v^{(k)}\);
  4. \(r^{(k)}=b-Ax^{(k)}\);
  5. if \(\ntwo{r^{(k)}}/\ntwo{b}\le\eps\), stop.

Steepest descent makes the same mistake repeatedly: on an ill-conditioned bowl it zig-zags across the narrow valley, each step partly undoing the last. Its residual directions ignore the history of directions already explored. Fixing this is the idea of conjugate gradient.

5.11 The conjugate gradient method

Imagine first \(A=I\): the level sets are spheres and steepest descent points straight at the minimum. More generally, if we could make our search directions orthogonal we would finish in \(n\) steps. The change of variables \(y=Bx\) with \(B^\top B=A\) (for instance \(B=A^{1/2}\)) turns \(g\) into \(\tilde g(y)=\tfrac12 y^\top y-\tilde b^\top y\), the identity-matrix problem — so the directions \(v^{(k)}\) should be orthogonal after transformation, i.e. \((Bv^{(k)})^\top(Bv^{(l)})=0\). Since \((Bv^{(k)})^\top(Bv^{(l)})=v^{(k)\top}A v^{(l)}\), the right notion is orthogonality in the \(A\)-inner product.

Definition 5.9 (A-orthogonality) For \(A\succ 0\) the \(A\)-inner product is \(\inner{u}{v}_A:=u^\top Av\). Vectors \(v^{(1)},\dots,v^{(m)}\) are \(A\)-orthogonal (conjugate) if \(\inner{v^{(k)}}{v^{(l)}}_A=0\) for all \(k\ne l\).

Conjugate gradient (CG) builds such directions on the fly. At step \(k\) it takes the steepest-descent starter \(r^{(k-1)}\) and \(A\)-orthogonalizes it against the previous directions by Gram–Schmidt in the \(A\)-inner product: \[ v^{(k)}=r^{(k-1)}-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \frac{\inner{r^{(k-1)}}{v^{(j)}}_A}{\inner{v^{(j)}}{v^{(j)}}_A}\,v^{(j)} . \] The remarkable fact — the engine of CG’s efficiency — is that in this construction the new residual \(r^{(k-1)}\) is already \(A\)-orthogonal to every earlier direction except the immediately preceding one, so the long sum collapses to a single term. The full orthogonalization then costs nothing beyond a short two-term recurrence, \[ v^{(1)}=r^{(0)},\qquad v^{(k)}=r^{(k-1)}+s^{(k-1)}v^{(k-1)},\qquad s^{(k-1)}=-\frac{\inner{r^{(k-1)}}{Av^{(k-1)}}}{\inner{v^{(k-1)}}{Av^{(k-1)}}} . \tag{5.18}\] The step length is the same exact line search Equation 5.17. One more trick keeps the cost at a single matrix–vector product (“matvec”) per iteration: never recompute \(Ax^{(k)}\) from scratch, but carry \(w^{(k)}:=Ax^{(k)}\) forward via \(w^{(k)}=w^{(k-1)}+t^{(k)}u^{(k)}\) where \(u^{(k)}:=Av^{(k)}\) is the one matvec of the step. Then \(r^{(k)}=b-w^{(k)}\) needs no new matvec.

NoteAlgorithm — Conjugate gradient (one matvec per step)

Given \(x^{(0)}\), \(b\), matvecs by \(A\succ 0\), a cap \(m\), and tolerance \(\eps\): compute \(w^{(0)}=Ax^{(0)}\), set \(r^{(0)}=b-w^{(0)}\). For \(k=1,2,\dots,m\):

  1. if \(k=1\), \(v^{(1)}=r^{(0)}\); else \(v^{(k)}=r^{(k-1)}+s^{(k-1)}v^{(k-1)}\);
  2. \(u^{(k)}=Av^{(k)}\) (the only matvec);
  3. \(t^{(k)}=\dfrac{\inner{v^{(k)}}{r^{(k-1)}}}{\inner{v^{(k)}}{u^{(k)}}}\);
  4. \(x^{(k)}=x^{(k-1)}+t^{(k)}v^{(k)}\) and \(w^{(k)}=w^{(k-1)}+t^{(k)}u^{(k)}\);
  5. \(r^{(k)}=b-w^{(k)}\); if \(\ntwo{r^{(k)}}/\ntwo{b}\le\eps\), stop;
  6. \(s^{(k)}=-\dfrac{\inner{r^{(k)}}{u^{(k)}}}{\inner{v^{(k)}}{u^{(k)}}}\).

The cost per iteration is therefore one matvec — the same as steepest descent — but the directions now account for the whole history, which is what buys the dramatically faster convergence of Figure 5.1. A minimal implementation of the loop:

import numpy as np

def conjugate_gradient(A, b, x0, m, eps):
    x = x0.copy()
    r = b - A @ x                    # residual
    nb = np.linalg.norm(b)
    v = r.copy()
    for k in range(m):
        if k > 0:
            v = r + s * v            # A-orthogonalize (two-term recurrence)
        u = A @ v                    # the one matvec
        t = (v @ r) / (v @ u)        # exact line search
        x = x + t * v
        r = r - t * u                # r = b - A x, updated without a new matvec
        if np.linalg.norm(r) / nb <= eps:
            break
        s = -(r @ u) / (v @ u)
    return x

5.12 Convergence of CG

Because the \(n\) directions CG produces are \(A\)-orthogonal, they form a basis and CG reaches the exact minimizer once it has searched all \(n\) of them.

Theorem 5.11 (Finite termination of CG) Applied to \(Ax=b\) with \(A\succ 0\), conjugate gradient recovers the exact solution in at most \(n\) iterations: \(x^{(k)}=x\) for all \(k\ge n\) (in exact arithmetic).

Proof. The search directions \(v^{(1)},\dots,v^{(n)}\) are \(A\)-orthogonal, hence linearly independent, hence a basis of \(\R^n\). Exact line search along each makes the error \(A\)-orthogonal to that direction, so after \(n\) steps the error is \(A\)-orthogonal to a full basis and must be zero. (Handwavy version of the source’s picture-proof.) \(\square\)

Finite termination alone already gives an \(\bigO(n^2)\) solver when each matvec costs \(\bigO(n)\) (as for sparse \(A\)), beating the \(\bigO(n^3)\) of a direct factorization. But the real power of CG is that it usually converges to good accuracy in far fewer than \(n\) iterations, at a rate set by the condition number.

Theorem 5.12 (Convergence rate of conjugate gradient) If \(A\succ 0\) has \(2\)-norm condition number \(\cond(A)=\cond_2(A)\), then the CG iterates for \(Ax=b\) satisfy \[ \ntwo{x^{(k)}-x}\le 2\,\cond(A)\left(\frac{\sqrt{\cond(A)}-1}{\sqrt{\cond(A)}+1}\right)^{k} \ntwo{x^{(0)}-x} . \tag{5.19}\]

Proof. Stated without proof (the standard argument bounds the \(A\)-norm of the error by a Chebyshev polynomial on the spectrum of \(A\)). For SPD \(A\), \(\cond_2(A)\) is the ratio of largest to smallest eigenvalue. The important reading is the base: when \(\cond(A)\) is large, \[ \frac{\sqrt{\cond(A)}-1}{\sqrt{\cond(A)}+1}\approx 1-\frac{2}{\sqrt{\cond(A)}}, \] so CG’s error decays geometrically at a rate governed by \(\sqrt{\cond(A)}\). \(\square\)

The contrast with the stationary methods is the whole point: Jacobi and Gauss–Seidel decay with base roughly \(1-2/\cond(A)\), whereas CG decays with base roughly \(1-2/\sqrt{\cond(A)}\) — a square-root improvement in the condition number that translates into far fewer iterations on ill-conditioned problems. Figure 5.1 shows the two descent methods on the same SPD system: steepest descent crawls while CG plunges.

5.13 Preconditioning

The rate Equation 5.19 says everything hinges on \(\cond(A)\): shrink it and CG converges faster. Preconditioning does exactly that by solving an equivalent, better-conditioned system. Suppose we can find \(C\) with \(A\approx CC^\top\) and with \(C\), \(C^\top\) easy to invert. Then \[ \tilde A:=C^{-1}AC^{-\top}\approx I \] is well-conditioned and still SPD, and \(A=C\tilde A C^\top\) gives the equivalent system \[ \tilde A\,\tilde x=\tilde b,\qquad \tilde x=C^\top x,\quad \tilde b=C^{-1}b . \tag{5.20}\] Preconditioned CG applies CG to Equation 5.20, then recovers \(x=C^{-\top}\tilde x\); each matvec by \(\tilde A\) is realized as a matvec by \(A\) sandwiched between solves with \(C^{-1}\) and \(C^{-\top}\). Finding \(C\) with \(C^{-1}AC^{-\top}\approx I\) and cheap solves is hard in general, and it is the central art of practical iterative solving. One cheap default is the diagonal (Jacobi) preconditioner \[ C=D^{1/2}, \] with \(D\) the diagonal of \(A\): then \(C^{-1}=C^{-\top}\) is a diagonal matrix with entries \(1/\sqrt{a_{ii}}\), and the preconditioned matrix \(\tilde A=(\tilde a_{ij})\) has \(\tilde a_{ii}=1\) for all \(i\) — a simple rescaling that already helps when \(A\)’s diagonal varies widely.

5.14 Chapter summary

  • Vector norms (Definition 5.1) and induced matrix norms (Definition 5.3) measure convergence; on \(\R^n\) all norms are equivalent (Theorem 5.2), and useful closed forms are \(\ninf{A}=\max_i\sum_j\abs{a_{ij}}\) (Theorem 5.3) and \(\ntwo{A}=\sigma_1(A)\) (Equation 5.4).
  • The spectral radius \(\spec(A)\) (Definition 5.4) satisfies \(\spec(A)\le\norm{A}\) and controls powers of \(A\): \(A\) is convergent iff \(\spec(A)<1\) (Theorem 5.4).
  • Jacobi (Equation 5.6) and Gauss–Seidel (Equation 5.8) split \(A=D-L-U\) and iterate \(x^{(k+1)}=Tx^{(k)}+c\); such an iteration converges for all data iff \(\spec(T)<1\) (Theorem 5.5), which holds whenever \(A\) is strictly diagonally dominant (Theorem 5.6), with rate \(\norm{T}^k\) (Corollary 5.1).
  • SOR (Equation 5.13) relaxes Gauss–Seidel by \(\omega\); convergence requires \(\omega\in(0,2)\)
    1. and is guaranteed there when \(A\succ 0\) (Theorem 5.8).
  • A small residual need not mean a small error; the gap is the condition number \(\cond(A)=\norm{A}\norm{A^{-1}}\ge 1\) (Definition 5.7, Theorem 5.9), matching
  • For \(A\succ 0\), solving \(Ax=b\) equals minimizing \(g(x)=\tfrac12x^\top Ax-b^\top x\) (Theorem 5.10). Steepest descent follows the residual with exact line search Equation 5.17 but zig-zags on ill-conditioned bowls.
