Default with Policy-Randomness Overestimation

Paper (PDF)
Abstract ▸

Why do some sovereigns pay high spreads even as their debt falls? Argentina did for years, a decoupling that standard models strain to explain. I study lenders who overweight the dispersion of a government’s policy choices (policy-randomness overestimation, PRO), a second-moment belief distortion. Every existing friction (reputation, ambiguity, diagnostic expectations) shifts the level of the spread schedule; PRO instead pivots its shape, raising the cost of debt at low balances and lowering it near the brink. The sovereign deleverages yet defaults at higher debt, so spreads persist as debt falls, a “stability illusion.” The sharp, falsifiable signature is a cross-sectional sign reversal: after a credibility shock, short and safe bonds widen while long and risky ones cheapen, which no level friction can produce. I discipline the friction with a detection-error probability rather than free-fitting it.

Policy and information extensions show limited power of fiscal transfers, persistence from negativity-biased learning, and welfare gains from transparency. Event-style hooks (e.g., Argentina’s inflation misreporting) align with the model’s pivot, threshold, and decoupling predictions. A rational-inattention microfoundation based on a mental-model-plus-residual representation endogenizes the tail weight within the same pricing operator.
